The History of Chinese Timber Architecture

Timber-frame buildings are found all over the China and are a unique symbol of Chinese architectural culture. Wooden components such as columns, beams, purlins, lintels, arches, etc. are connected by mortise and tenon joints, which are flexible and earthquake-resistant. By assembling pre-fabricated components, a sturdy frame can be quickly installed at the building site. In addition to structural carpentry, the building process includes decorative carpentry, tile roofing, stonework, decorative painting, and other arts taught orally and practically by masters and apprentices. Each stage of the construction process demonstrates its unique and systematic methods and skills. Traditional Chinese wood building crafts are nowadays mainly used in the construction of traditional style buildings and the restoration of ancient wood buildings, reflecting the inheritance of wisdom and craftsmanship, and the inherited understanding of traditional Chinese people of nature and human relationships. Society. For the carpenters and craftsmen who have preserved this style of architecture, and for the people who have lived in the spaces it defines for generations, it has become a central visual component of Chinese identity and an important representation of Asian architecture.

If categorized according to time, Chinese wooden buildings could be divided into three periods--“Period of Vigor”，“Period of Elegance”，“Period of Rigidity”.

Period of Vigor

The Period of Vigor was mainly in the Tang Dynasty, the bulidings were the oldest surviving Chinese timber building, built around 600-1050. Some famous buildings of this period were the Guanyin Pavilion, Dulesi and "Buddha Light Monastery" Foguangsi.

Period of Elegance

The Period of Elegance was mainly in the Song Dynasty, built around 1000-1400. A famous book Yingzao Fashi was published by 1103. It was compiled by Li Jie, superintendent of construction at the court of emperor Song Huizong. Some famous buildings of this period were the Moni Hall of Longxing-si, Sage Mother Hall of Jin-ci, and Main Hall of Chuzu-an at Shaolin-si.

Period of Rigidity

The Period of Rigidity was mainly in the Ming and Qing Dynasty, built around 1400-1900. The Ming-Qing Standards were Gongcheng zuofa(Regulations for Architectrual Design) and Qingshi yingzao zeli(Qing Structural Regulations). Some famous buildings of this period were Changling (Tomb of Yongle Emperor), Taihedian in the Forbidden City, and Dacheng Hall, Temple of Confucius.
